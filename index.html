What is Climbing?

Climbing is something we've all done at some point, be it climbing the stairs, a nice-looking tree, or the corporate ladder.

Climbing rocks as an outdoor activity has been a leisure activity for centuries, but we're looking at modern styles of climbing and some of the hardest forms it has taken so far.

Traditional Climbing

Within the traditional climbing discipline, climbers carry protective gear on their harness and place it into features of the rock to catch them if they fall. These pieces of protection are most commonly nuts, hexes, and cams. Nuts and hexes are passive protection; static pieces that simply wedge into the rock. Cams are a bit fancier, and feature 4 lobes. In their default position, lobes are widest. There is a spring-loaded lever which will pull the lobes downwards, making them narrower. These are then placed into a crack, and the outwards force of the cam keeps it in place in the rock.

This image is of a climb named The Walk of Life. It was first climbed in 2008 by James Pearson, who declared this climb to be the hardest trad climb in the world, and gave it the grade E12 (the E means extreme). Another climber, Dave MacLeod, climbed this in January 2009, and decimated James Person's grade, knocking the difficulty down to E9.

Sport Climbing

Sport climbing is the most accessible form of rope climbing. Holes are drilled into a rockface and bolts glued in, to allow carabiners to be easily and securely attached.

This climber is named Adam Ondra, who was the first climber to set a grade of 9C.

Sport climbing grades are different to traditional grades, and boulder grades. His 9C is a route named Silence in a gorgeous cave in Flatanger, Norway. It took him several years to successfully do this, and he is considered the best climber in the world today.

Bouldering
